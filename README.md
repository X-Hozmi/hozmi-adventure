
The year is 1347.  Dust swirled around the hooves of my mare as we crested the hill, the vast, unforgiving landscape of the French countryside stretching before us.  Ahead lay the Pyrenees, a formidable barrier between me and my destination, Santiago de Compostela.  Each day was a gamble; bandits lurked in shadowed forests, rivers swelled unexpectedly, and the relentless sun beat down mercilessly.  But the whispered promise of the holy city, of finding solace and perhaps even a miracle, fueled my weary bones and spurred me onward through the treacherous wilderness.


Conversely, in 2742, the hum of the Althea's hyperdrive was a constant companion as we leaped across the void between galaxies.  The shimmering nebulae outside my viewport were breathtaking, ephemeral brushstrokes across the black canvas of space. Our mission: to survey the newly discovered Kepler-186f system.  The risks were different – asteroid fields, rogue gravitational wells, and the ever-present threat of encountering unknown alien lifeforms.  Yet, the thrill of exploration, the potential for discovery, surpassed the dangers, and propelled our crew towards the unknown.
